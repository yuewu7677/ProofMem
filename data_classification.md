# Data Classification Rubric for Proof Knowledge Levels

This document rigorously defines four proof knowledge levels (L1–L4) used to classify evaluation instances in formal theorem proving. These levels characterize the relationship between evaluation theorems and training data, focusing on measurable criteria rather than subjective proof quality.

---

## L1. Replay

### Goal  
Evaluate the model’s ability to reproduce proofs for exact theorem statements seen during training.

### Formal definition  
- The evaluation theorem statement is *identical* to one in the training corpus.  
- The statement hash, computed after whitespace normalization, matches exactly.  
- The model generates a proof that passes Lean verification *after* training.

### Inclusion criteria (measurable)  
- The theorem statement string matches a training example exactly modulo whitespace.  
- The generated proof is accepted by Lean.

### Exclusion criteria  
- Any theorem statement differing from training by any syntactic change.  
- Proofs generated *before* training.

### Automatic generation procedure  
- Extract theorem-proof pairs directly from the training corpus.  
- Filter duplicates by statement hash.

### Human verification checklist  
- Confirm the statement identity with training data.  
- Confirm that the proof is Lean-verified.

### Metrics  
- pass@1, pass@k (success rates under different budgets)  
- Proof length (number of proof steps)  
- Generation time (inference and search duration)

### Example  
- Theorem: `theorem pow_orderOf' (a : G) : a ^ orderOf a = 1 := by ...`  
- Proof generated by the model exactly matches this statement and Lean accepts it.

---

## L2. Theorem Perturbation

### Goal  
Measure robustness to surface-level syntactic and presentation changes while preserving mathematical equivalence.

### Formal definition  
- The evaluation theorem is *mathematically equivalent* to an L1 theorem.  
- Their abstract syntax trees (ASTs) are equivalent modulo:  
  - Alpha-renaming of variables and binders  
  - Reordering of binders or hypotheses  
  - Commutativity and associativity of connectives  
  - Symmetry of equality  
  - Definitional unfolding or folding  
- The original theorem statement is *not* allowed verbatim.

### Inclusion criteria (measurable)  
- The perturbed theorem passes a semantic equivalence check against an L1 theorem.  
- The proof generated is Lean-verified.

### Exclusion criteria  
- Any theorem statement identical to an L1 statement.  
- Statements that change mathematical meaning.

### Automatic generation procedure  
- Apply systematic perturbations to L1 theorems, including:  
  - Variable renaming  
  - Hypothesis permutation  
  - Rewriting goals using existing equivalence lemmas  
  - Changing equality orientation (e.g., `a = b` to `b = a`)  
  - Introducing or removing intermediate `let` bindings

### Human verification checklist  
- Verify semantic equivalence of perturbed and original theorems.  
- Confirm that the proof is valid and Lean-verified.

### Metrics  
- Perturbation robustness = pass(L2) / pass(L1)

### Example  
- Base theorem: `theorem pow_orderOf' (a : G) : a ^ orderOf a = 1 := by ...`  
- Perturbed theorem: `theorem pow_orderOf_renamed' (x : G) : 1 = x ^ orderOf x := by ...`  
- Proof may differ but must prove an equivalent statement.

---

## L3. Sibling-Theorem Transfer

### Goal  
Evaluate the model’s ability to generalize proof ideas to new but related theorems outside training data.

### Formal definition  
- The evaluation theorem is *not logically equivalent* to any training theorem.  
- It belongs to the same *theorem family* (e.g., similar domain, concepts, or namespace).  
- Shares *at least one major proof ingredient* with a training theorem, such as:  
  - Induction steps  
  - Cancellation or rewriting patterns  
  - Extensionality arguments  
  - Simplification or normalization techniques

### Inclusion criteria (measurable)  
- The theorem is distinct from training theorems by logical equivalence.  
- Proven by reusing proof strategies or tactics common to the theorem family.  
- Proof is Lean-verified.

### Exclusion criteria  
- Theorems logically equivalent to training statements.  
- Theorems from unrelated domains or proof strategies.

### Automatic generation procedure  
- Identify theorem families via clustering, dependency graphs, or namespaces.  
- Generate siblings by exploring neighbors in theorem-family graphs or shared tactic signatures.

### Human verification checklist  
- Confirm the theorem is novel and not equivalent to training data.  
- Verify that the proof strategy overlaps with known family strategies.  
- Confirm Lean verification.

### Metrics  
- Sibling transfer rate = pass(L3) / number of L3 candidates

### Example  
- Training theorem: `gcd_comm' (m n : ℕ) : Nat.gcd m n = Nat.gcd n m`  
- Sibling theorem: `gcd_assoc' (m n k : ℕ) : Nat.gcd (Nat.gcd m n) k = Nat.gcd m (Nat.gcd n k)`  
- Proof reuses reasoning about gcd properties, but the statement is distinct.

---

## L4. Cross-Domain Retention

### Goal  
Measure how well proof knowledge from an initial domain is retained after training on a disjoint domain.

### Formal definition  
- Training proceeds sequentially: first domain A, then domain B.  
- Domains A and B are *disjoint* by namespace and dependency thresholds.  
- Evaluate the model on domain A theorems *after* training on domain B.

### Inclusion criteria (measurable)  
- Domains A and B have no overlapping namespaces or minimal dependency overlap.  
- Theorems from domain A are evaluated before and after domain B training.  
- Proofs are Lean-verified.

### Exclusion criteria  
- Domains with significant overlap in namespaces or dependencies.  
- Theorems not verified by Lean.

### Automatic generation procedure  
- Partition the corpus into disjoint domains by namespace and dependency graph analysis.  
- Train sequentially on domain A then domain B.  
- Evaluate on domain A test set before and after domain B training.

### Human verification checklist  
- Confirm domain disjointness.  
- Confirm Lean verification of proofs at both stages.

### Metrics  
- Retention = pass rate on domain A after domain B training / pass rate before domain B training  
- Forgetting = pass rate before - pass rate after  
- Forward transfer on domain B = pass rate on B after training / baseline on B before training

### Example  
- Domain A: group theory lemmas such as `mul_inv_rev'` and `inv_one'`  
- Domain B: number theory lemmas such as `dvd_add'` and `gcd_dvd_left'`  
- Evaluate group theory proofs after number theory fine-tuning.

## Automatic Dataset Construction Pipeline

1. Collect a large corpus of theorem-proof pairs with metadata.  
2. Deduplicate theorems by normalized statement hashes.  
3. Cluster theorems into families using dependency graphs and namespaces.  
4. Generate L2 perturbations automatically from L1 theorems using syntax transformations.  
5. Generate L3 sibling theorems by exploring theorem-family graphs and tactic signatures.  
6. Split the corpus into disjoint domains for L4 evaluation based on namespaces and dependency thresholds.  
7. Run Lean verification on all generated proofs to ensure correctness.  
8. Assign rich metadata for tracking provenance, transformations, and verification status.

---

## Metadata Schema

```
id                  # Unique identifier
level               # L1, L2, L3, or L4
source_dataset      # Original dataset or corpus name
domain              # Mathematical domain or namespace
theorem_family      # Cluster or family identifier
training_split      # Train/validation/test split label
parent_theorem      # For L2/L3, the original theorem id
transformation      # Description of perturbation or sibling generation
proof_strategy      # High-level tactics or proof ingredients used
lean_version        # Lean version used for verification
mathlib_version     # Mathlib version used
verified            # Boolean indicating Lean verification success
```

## Saturation / initial-correctness control

In ProofMem, "unsaturated" means that the base model should not already solve most examples before ProofMem training or adaptation. It does **not** only mean private, unseen, or newly authored.

The target band for the main experiments should be:

- `too_easy`: baseline Lean-verified correctness is high; useful only for smoke tests  
- `target_unsaturated`: baseline correctness is low-to-moderate; primary ProofMem data  
- `too_hard`: baseline correctness is near zero; useful only for stress tests or later stages

A practical first target is:

- pass@1 approximately 10--30%, or  
- pass@k approximately 20--50% under the intended inference/search budget

Examples around 0--5% are usually too hard for the first ProofMem experiment, because they give too few successful proofs for replay and make it difficult to distinguish training effects from new learning. Examples above 60% are too saturated, because they leave little headroom.

Therefore, dataset selection should be empirical. For each candidate dataset, run the intended base model under the intended inference budget and record:

- baseline pass@1  
- baseline pass@k  
- Lean version and Mathlib version  
- generation budget and search/correction budget  
- number of verified successful proofs  
- average successful proof length  
- whether proofs depend on retrieval, premise selection, external search, or self-correction

## Recommended ProofMem dataset plan

Do not use one dataset as the whole project. Use a multi-domain benchmark suite with empirical filtering:

1. **Primary public candidate pool:** FormalMATH, because it is broad, Lean 4, public, and has low published baseline correctness.  
2. **Graduate/research slices:** FormalProofBench if access is available; otherwise LeanCat, FATE-H/FATE-X, and ArXivLean as smaller high-level slices.  
3. **Training/replay source:** Lean Workbook and LEAN-GitHub-style corpora for theorem-proof pairs, because ProofMem needs verified proof scripts for L1 replay.  
4. **Calibration/stress tests:** PutnamBench and CombiBench.

The first concrete experiment should be:

- choose a strong open Lean prover as the base model;  
- run it on the candidate pool;  
- keep examples whose initial correctness falls in the target band;  
- use the verified successful proofs as L1 replay examples;  
- generate L2 perturbations and L3 siblings only for examples that pass the Lean checker;  
- evaluate L4 retention by training or adapting across domains.
